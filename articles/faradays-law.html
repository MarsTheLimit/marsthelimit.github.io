Faraday’s Law, Capacitance, and Capacitors for Beginners

Posted on June 24, 2025 by Boden Bensema

Concept

Laws

Capacitors and magnetic induction are two key building blocks of electronics. Whether you're working with circuits, motors, or sensors, understanding how capacitance and Faraday's Law work can help you design and troubleshoot with confidence. This article breaks these concepts down for beginners, step by step.

What is Capacitance?

Capacitance is a fundamental property of every electrical circuit. Technically, capacitance refers to a component or system's ability to store electric charge when a voltage is applied to it, usually in capacitors.

Even when you don't intentionally add a capacitor to a circuit, small amounts of capacitance can still appear. This is called stray capacitance, and it happens when two conductors, like copper wires, are close together and separated by an insulator (like air or plastic). These small electric fields can unintentionally store and transfer energy. This usually is not a problem in low-speed circuits, but in high-speed applications, the signals decay, and your circuit may fail. It's important to use shorter wires whenever possible to limit the stray capacitance.

Capacitance is measured in farads (F). Typically, 1 farad is quite large for typical capacitor applications. Some more common measurements are microfarads (μF), nanofarads (nF), or picofarads (pF). The formula for calculating capacitance is below:

C = Q/V

Where

  • C is capacitance in farads
  • Q is the charge stored in coulombs
  • V is the voltage in volts

What are Coulombs?

A coulomb measures how much electric charge flows. One ampere of current means one coulomb of charge is flowing per second. Essentially, a coulomb is a measurement of how many electric charges (electrons or protons) are moving. One coulomb equals approximately 6.24 • 1018 electrons. Unlike energy (measured in joules), coulombs measure how much charge flows through a circuit. For example, if a 47μF capacitor is charged with 5V,

 .000047 • 5 = .000235 C

The capacitor used .000235 coulombs. This equation was derived from the C = Q/V to get CV = Q.

What Is Faraday's Law of Induction?

Faraday's law of induction states that a changing magnetic field creates an electromotive force, or voltage, in a circuit. This may seem daunting, but you have most likely seen this law at work in real life. For example, let's take a look at an induction motor, also called a generator.

When I spin the motor, it generates electricity by a changing magnetic field near a copper wire (or the circuit). Using this law, you can also generate electricity by passing a magnet through a pipe that has copper wire wound into a coil on the outside of the pipe.

How does Faraday's Law of Induction Work?

Electrical current, at a subatomic level, is electrons being “pushed” around inside a conductor. When a magnetic field is moved close to a conductor, the field pulls the electrons with the magnet, and this generates current. This law is important to our everyday lives, as most of the power in the world is generated using techniques involving this law.

What are Capacitors Used For in Real Life?

Capacitors can be charged and discharged quickly, making them useful for balancing power flow, filtering signals, and timing operations (such as with the 555 timer) in electronic circuits.

However, if you want to have the capacitor slowly charge and discharge, you can attach a resistor to one of the capacitor's terminals. Some more interesting things capacitors are used for:

  • Camera flashes
  • Tasers
  • AC to DC conversion
  • Temporary energy storage

Capacitors have many uses and are one of the fundamental electrical components. Check out our article about using capacitors in real life for more information on how to use these components yourself.

People also ask

What is capacitance in simple terms?
Capacitance is the ability of a component or material to store electrical charge. In simple terms, it's like how a water balloon stores water — a capacitor stores electricity and can release it when needed.
What does a capacitor do in a circuit?
A capacitor stores and releases electrical energy. It can smooth out voltage, filter electrical signals, and help with timing. In a power supply, for example, capacitors help keep the voltage steady and reduce noise.
What is a coulomb?
A coulomb (C) is a unit of electric charge. One coulomb equals the amount of charge transferred when a current of one ampere flows for one second.
What is Faraday's Law of Induction?
Faraday's Law says that a changing magnetic field can create a voltage in a nearby wire or circuit. It's the principle behind generators, transformers, and wireless charging.
